Paper §5.3

Adding a multiple of an equation to another row produces an equivalent model. If the multiple is chosen to cancel coefficients that the two rows share, the result is a sparser row.

Concretely, suppose AqS​xS​+AqT​xT​+AqU​xU​ArS​xS​+ArT​xT​+ArV​xV​​=bq​∘r​br​​ where S,T,U,V partition the joint support and AqS​ is parallel to ArS​ via s, i.e. sAqS​=ArS​. Subtracting s times the equation from the inequality eliminates the S-block from the second row, leaving (ArT​−sAqT​)xT​−sAqU​xU​+ArV​xV​∘r​br​−sbq​. The new row has ∣U∣ extra non-zeros from the U-block but ∣S∣ fewer from S, so the operation is profitable when ∣S∣>∣U∣.

The motivation is computational: many MIP solver subroutines scale with the number of non-zeros in the matrix, so a sparser model often runs faster even at constant feasible region. A side benefit is that the cancellation may produce singleton columns or rows, which then unlock further presolve.

The scalar s used in the cancellation must be moderate (∣s∣ not too large), or finite-precision arithmetic on the new coefficients introduces numerical issues that hurt later LP solves.
