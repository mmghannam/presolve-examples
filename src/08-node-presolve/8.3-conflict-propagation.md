> Paper §8.3

When a node's LP relaxation is infeasible, the dual ray of the failed simplex provides a *Farkas certificate* — a non-negative combination $y \geq 0$ such that $y^T A \leq y^T b$ contradicts the local bounds. Multiplying through and rewriting yields a **conflict constraint** that holds for the original (root) model — not just the failed sub-problem — and that excludes the configuration of bounds that caused the infeasibility.

Two flavors of conflict constraint are useful:

- **Bound-disjunction constraints.** If the bounds in the conflict are all on binary variables, the conflict has the form $\bigvee_k (x_k = \bar v_k)$. These are propagated efficiently using the *two-watched-literals* technique borrowed from SAT solving — only two variables in each clause are actively watched, and the clause is only revisited when one of them is fixed.
- **Linear conflict constraints.** When non-binary variables are involved, the conflict is a linear inequality $y^T A x \leq y^T b$. These are kept as regular constraints in the model and propagated like any other row.

Conflict constraints are useful primarily in the local neighborhood of the node where they were derived; their global utility decays quickly as branching moves the search elsewhere. Implementations therefore cap the number of conflict constraints retained, evicting the oldest or least-useful ones when the cap is hit.
