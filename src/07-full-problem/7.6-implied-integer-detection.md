A continuous variable $x_j$ is **implied integer** if every feasible solution to the model satisfies $x_j \in \mathbb{Z}$, even though the model does not declare $x_j$ as integer. Detecting this is useful: implied-integer variables can be branched on, used to derive stronger cuts (mixed-integer rounding cuts behave differently for integer vs. continuous variables), and exploited by domain propagation.

Two detection methods cover most cases:

- **Primal detection.** If $x_j$ appears in an equation $A_{iS} x_S + a_{ij} x_j = b_i$ with $S$ entirely composed of integer or already-implied-integer variables, and $b_i / a_{ij} \in \mathbb{Z}$ and $a_{is} / a_{ij} \in \mathbb{Z}$ for all $s \in S$, then $a_{ij} x_j = b_i - A_{iS} x_S \in \mathbb{Z}$, so $x_j$ takes only integer values.

- **Dual detection.** If the LP has an optimal vertex solution and the integer variables are fixed, the continuous variables form a vertex of the resulting "fixed LP". For a continuous $x_j$ that appears only in inequalities, the vertex condition forces at least one of those inequalities to be tight, or $x_j$ to be at one of its bounds. If the integer-fixed inequalities are themselves integer (after fixing) and $\ell_j, u_j \in \mathbb{Z}$, then $x_j$ must take an integer value.

Once $x_j$ is marked implied integer, downstream cut generation, branching, and propagation treat it as integer — though the model formulation is unchanged.
