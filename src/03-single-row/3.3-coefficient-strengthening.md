# 3.3 Coefficient strengthening

> Paper §3.3

## The reduction

Take an inequality with an **integer** variable $x_k$:
$$A_{iS}\,x_S + a_{ik}\,x_k \leq b_i, \qquad S = \operatorname{supp}(A_{i\cdot}) \setminus \{k\},\ x_k \in \mathbb{Z}.$$
Assume $a_{ik} > 0$ and $u_k < \infty$. Let
$$u_{iS} := \sup\{A_{iS}\,x_S\} = \sum_{j \in S,\ a_{ij} > 0} a_{ij}\, u_j + \sum_{j \in S,\ a_{ij} < 0} a_{ij}\, \ell_j$$
be the *maximum* activity of the rest of the row (the mirror image of the $\ell_{iS}$ used in [§3.2](3.2-bound-strengthening.md)).

Now compute
$$d := b_i - u_{iS} - a_{ik}(u_k - 1).$$
This is the **slack** of the constraint at $x_k = u_k - 1$ when the rest of the row is at its maximum activity. If $d > 0$ and $a_{ik} \geq d$, the constraint can be replaced by the **strengthened** version
$$A_{iS}\,x_S + (a_{ik} - d)\,x_k \leq b_i - d\,u_k. \tag{$\star$}$$

### Why this works

Compare the two constraints at each integer value of $x_k$:

| $x_k$ | Original RHS for $A_{iS}x_S$ | Strengthened RHS for $A_{iS}x_S$ |
|---|---|---|
| $u_k$ | $b_i - a_{ik}\,u_k$ | $(b_i - d\,u_k) - (a_{ik} - d)\,u_k = b_i - a_{ik}\,u_k$ |
| $u_k - 1$ | $b_i - a_{ik}(u_k - 1)$ | $(b_i - d\,u_k) - (a_{ik} - d)(u_k - 1) = b_i - a_{ik}(u_k - 1) - d = u_{iS}$ |
| $\leq u_k - 2$ | even looser | even looser |

At $x_k = u_k$ the two constraints say exactly the same thing. At $x_k = u_k - 1$ the strengthened one says "$A_{iS}\,x_S \leq u_{iS}$" — which is automatically satisfied because $u_{iS}$ is the maximum possible activity of $A_{iS}\,x_S$. The integer-feasible region is therefore unchanged, but the LP relaxation is **strictly tighter**: any LP point with $x_k$ fractional in $(u_k - 1, u_k)$ is now cut off if it relied on the original constraint's slack.

The key insight: the original constraint was "loose at $x_k = u_k - 1$ but not at $x_k = u_k$", and the strengthening levels the two cases by transferring slack from the coefficient on $x_k$ to the right-hand side.

The mirror case ($a_{ik} < 0$, tightening at the lower bound) follows the same recipe with signs swapped.

## What fires

Apply the rule with $k = z$.

- $a_{ik} = 5 > 0$, $u_k = 1$.
- $S = \{x, y\}$, $A_{iS} = (2, 3)$. Both coefficients are positive, so
  $$u_{iS} = 2 \cdot u_x + 3 \cdot u_y = 2 \cdot 1 + 3 \cdot 1 = 5.$$
- Slack at $x_k = u_k - 1 = 0$:
  $$d = b_i - u_{iS} - a_{ik}(u_k - 1) = 8 - 5 - 5 \cdot 0 = 3.$$
- $d = 3 > 0$ ✓ and $a_{ik} = 5 \geq d = 3$ ✓ — both conditions hold.

Apply $(\star)$:
- new coefficient on $z$: $5 - 3 = 2$,
- new right-hand side: $8 - 3 \cdot 1 = 5$.

The strengthened constraint is
$$2z + 2x + 3y \leq 5.$$

Sanity-check the integer cases:

- $z = 0$: original gives $2x + 3y \leq 8$, new gives $2x + 3y \leq 5$. Since $\sup\{2x + 3y\} = 5$, both are satisfied by every feasible $(x, y) \in [0, 1]^2$ — the integer-feasible region is the same.
- $z = 1$: original gives $2x + 3y \leq 3$, new gives $2x + 3y \leq 3$ — identical.

The LP cut is real: $(z, x, y) = (0.5, 1, 1)$ was LP-feasible originally ($5 \cdot 0.5 + 2 + 3 = 7.5 \leq 8$) and is no longer feasible ($2 \cdot 0.5 + 2 + 3 = 6 > 5$).

## Notes

- **Integrality is essential.** The strengthening exploits the gap between $x_k = u_k - 1$ and $x_k = u_k$. For a continuous $x_k$ no such gap exists, and the constraint $A_{i\cdot}x \leq b_i$ is already as tight as it can be in the LP.
- **When $a_{ik} = d$.** The new coefficient on $x_k$ becomes $0$, so $x_k$ disappears from the row entirely. The constraint shrinks in support. This is more aggressive than just changing a number — it can trigger downstream singleton-column or [implied-free](../04-single-column/4.5-implied-free-substitution.md) substitutions.
- **When $a_{ik} < d$.** The bare strengthening formula would produce a negative coefficient on $x_k$, which is valid but unhelpful. In this case the original constraint is so loose at $x_k = u_k - 1$ that bound strengthening ([§3.2](3.2-bound-strengthening.md)) on $x_k$ from the activity at $x_k = u_k$ gives a tighter result, so coefficient strengthening yields to that.
- **LP solvers cannot do this on their own.** The strengthened constraint is a valid inequality for the integer-feasible region but *not* for the LP relaxation — it cuts off LP-feasible points. An LP solver, with no knowledge of integrality, has no reason to introduce it. The reduction is therefore an MIP-presolve speciality, valuable precisely because the LP relaxation gets stronger for free.
