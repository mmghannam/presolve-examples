> Paper §3.7

A **SOS1** constraint requires at most one variable in $\{x_1, \ldots, x_p\}$ to be non-zero. A **SOS2** constraint requires at most two, and any two non-zero variables must be adjacent in the specified order. Looking at a single SOS constraint in isolation, several simplifications follow directly from the variables' bounds and integrality:

- **Fixing.** A SOS1 with a member $x_k$ for which $\ell_k > 0$ or $u_k < 0$ forces $x_k$ to be the (only) non-zero one, so every other member must be fixed to zero.
- **Removing zero-fixed members.** Variables already fixed to zero can be deleted from a SOS1. For SOS2 the deletion is restricted to members at the start or end of the sequence — deleting an interior zero-fixed member would break the adjacency relation that defines the constraint.
- **Trivializing.** A SOS1 reduced to one remaining variable, or a SOS2 reduced to at most two remaining variables, no longer says anything and can be discarded.
- **SOS2 → SOS1.** A SOS2 over exactly three variables $(x_1, x_2, x_3)$ reduces to a SOS1 over $(x_1, x_3)$, since adjacency forces the middle position to be the one non-zero singleton if any.
- **Discarding length-2 SOS1.** A SOS1 of length 2 can be discarded entirely if the objective and constraint signs allow both members to be jointly decreased to zero in post-processing without making the model worse.

These are the only reductions available from a single SOS constraint; richer reasoning across SOS constraints belongs to the multi-row part.
