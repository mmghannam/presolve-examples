> Paper §6.3

Two columns $A_{\cdot j}$ and $A_{\cdot k}$ (with $j \neq k$) are **parallel** if there is a scalar $\lambda \neq 0$ with $A_{\cdot k} = \lambda\, A_{\cdot j}$. If, additionally, the objective coefficients satisfy $c_k = \lambda\, c_j$ and the integrality / bound types are compatible, the two variables can be merged into a single new variable
$$y := x_j + \lambda\, x_k.$$
Every constraint sees only the *combination* $a_{ij}(x_j + \lambda x_k) = a_{ij} y$, and the objective sees only $c_j (x_j + \lambda x_k) = c_j y$, so the model can be rewritten in $y$ alone.

The bounds on $y$ depend on $\lambda$ and the bounds of $x_j, x_k$:
$$\ell_y = \begin{cases} \ell_j + \lambda \ell_k & \lambda > 0 \\ \ell_j + \lambda u_k & \lambda < 0 \end{cases}, \qquad
u_y = \begin{cases} u_j + \lambda u_k & \lambda > 0 \\ u_j + \lambda \ell_k & \lambda < 0. \end{cases}$$

Integer variables require extra care: even if the merged $y$ is integer, splitting $y^\star$ back into integer $x_j^\star, x_k^\star$ at uncrush time may not be possible unless the image of $x_j + \lambda x_k$ over the variables' domains is an interval of integers without holes. A sufficient condition is $|\lambda(u_k - \ell_k)| \geq 1$ when one of the variables is integer and the other continuous.

Paper Example 8 — the LP $\min 2x_1 + 4x_2 + x_3$ with column 2 parallel to column 1 via $\lambda = 2$ — is a clean instance.
