# 6.1 Multi-column dual fixing for single equations

> Paper §6.1

Single-column dual fixing (§4.4) only applies to variables that do not appear in any equation. **Multi-column dual fixing** generalizes the argument to recover a similar conclusion when the variable participates in *exactly one* equation $r$, as long as enough other variables in that equation can absorb its movement.

Concretely: let $S_+ \subseteq N$ be the variables $s$ in row $r$ with $c_s \geq 0$, $a_{is} \geq 0$ on every $\leq$-row, and $a_{is} = 0$ on every other equation. These are the variables that "move freely upward" in the single equation $r$. Splitting $S_-$ analogously and letting $S_-^C$ be the continuous variables among them, the condition
$$\inf\{A_{rT_-} x_{T_-}\} + \sup\{A_{rS_-^C} x_{S_-^C}\} \geq b_r$$
(where $T_- = N \setminus S_-^C$) certifies that the variables in $S_+$ can be fixed to their lower bounds without violating row $r$ — any decrease in row $r$'s activity from doing so can be compensated by increasing the continuous variables in $S_-^C$.

The structural conditions are demanding, so the reduction triggers on a small fraction of models. When it does fire, it can fix many variables at once.
