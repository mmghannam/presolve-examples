# 5.2 Parallel and nearly parallel rows

> Paper §5.2

Two rows $q$ and $r$ are **parallel** if there is a scalar $s \neq 0$ with $A_{q\cdot} = s\, A_{r\cdot}$. Several merges follow from the relationship between $b_q$ and $s\, b_r$:

- **Two equations.** $r$ is discarded if $b_q = s\, b_r$; otherwise the two equations are inconsistent and the model is infeasible.
- **One equation, one inequality.** The inequality is implied if its right-hand side is on the loose side of the equation (sign-dependent on $s$); on the tight side, the model is infeasible.
- **Two inequalities.** If $s > 0$, the looser of the two is implied by the tighter and is discarded. If $s < 0$, the two inequalities together collapse to a *ranged row* $sb_r \leq A_{q\cdot}x \leq b_q$ (or, if the bounds coincide, an equation), and the second inequality is removed.

A useful generalization handles **nearly parallel** rows that differ only on singleton variables: one can substitute the singleton out (turning the rows into truly parallel rows of strictly smaller support) and then apply the above rules. The substitution requires tightening the singleton's bounds first, which is itself a useful side effect.

Detection is by hashing the row's support and normalized coefficients into buckets and comparing only within each bucket.
