Presolve Reductions in MIP — Worked Examples

3.3 Coefficient strengthening

Paper §3.3

The reduction

Take an inequality with an integer variable xk​: AiS​xS​+aik​xk​≤bi​,S=supp(Ai⋅​)∖{k}, xk​∈Z. Assume aik​>0 and uk​<∞. Let uiS​:=sup{AiS​xS​}=j∈S, aij​>0∑​aij​uj​+j∈S, aij​<0∑​aij​ℓj​ be the maximum activity of the rest of the row (the mirror image of the ℓiS​ used in §3.2).

Now compute d:=bi​−uiS​−aik​(uk​−1). This is the slack of the constraint at xk​=uk​−1 when the rest of the row is at its maximum activity. If d>0 and aik​≥d, the constraint can be replaced by the strengthened version AiS​xS​+(aik​−d)xk​≤bi​−duk​.(⋆)

Why this works

Compare the two constraints at each integer value of xk​:

xk​Original RHS for AiS​xS​Strengthened RHS for AiS​xS​
uk​bi​−aik​uk​(bi​−duk​)−(aik​−d)uk​=bi​−aik​uk​
uk​−1bi​−aik​(uk​−1)(bi​−duk​)−(aik​−d)(uk​−1)=bi​−aik​(uk​−1)−d=uiS​

At xk​=uk​ the two constraints say exactly the same thing. At xk​=uk​−1 the strengthened one says "AiS​xS​≤uiS​" — which is automatically satisfied because uiS​ is the maximum possible activity of AiS​xS​. The integer-feasible region is therefore unchanged, but the LP relaxation is strictly tighter: any LP point with xk​ fractional in (uk​−1,uk​) is now cut off if it relied on the original constraint's slack.

The key insight: the original constraint was "loose at xk​=uk​−1 but not at xk​=uk​", and the strengthening levels the two cases by transferring slack from the coefficient on xk​ to the right-hand side.

The mirror case (aik​<0, tightening at the lower bound) follows the same recipe with signs swapped.

Instance

\ §3.3 Coefficient strengthening
\
\ The binary variable z has coefficient 5 in the constraint.
\ The "rest of the row" 2x + 3y has maximum activity 2 + 3 = 5 over the bounds.
\ At z = 0 the constraint reads 2x + 3y <= 8 but the LHS can be at most 5,
\ so the constraint has 3 units of slack at z = 0.
\ Strengthening shrinks z's coefficient from 5 to 2 and the RHS from 8 to 5.

What fires

Apply the rule with k=z.

  • aik​=5>0, uk​=1.
  • S={x,y}, AiS​=(2,3). Both coefficients are positive, so uiS​=2⋅ux​+3⋅uy​=2⋅1+3⋅1=5.
  • Slack at xk​=uk​−1=0: d=bi​−uiS​−aik​(uk​−1)=8−5−5⋅0=3.
  • d=3>0 ✓ and aik​=5≥d=3 ✓ — both conditions hold.

Apply (⋆):

  • new coefficient on z: 5−3=2,
  • new right-hand side: 8−3⋅1=5.

The strengthened constraint is 2z+2x+3y≤5.

Post-presolve model

\ After coefficient strengthening on c1 against the binary variable z.
\
\ New coefficient on z:  a'_z = 5 - 3 = 2
\ New right-hand side:   b' = 8 - 3 * 1 = 5

\ The integer-feasible region is unchanged. The LP relaxation is strictly
\ tighter — e.g., the point (z, x, y) = (0.5, 1, 1) was LP-feasible in the
\ original constraint (5*0.5 + 2 + 3 = 7.5 <= 8) and is now cut off

Sanity-check the integer cases:

  • z=0: original gives 2x+3y≤8, new gives 2x+3y≤5. Since sup{2x+3y}=5, both are satisfied by every feasible (x,y)∈[0,1]2 — the integer-feasible region is the same.
  • z=1: original gives 2x+3y≤3, new gives 2x+3y≤3 — identical.

The LP cut is real: (z,x,y)=(0.5,1,1) was LP-feasible originally (5⋅0.5+2+3=7.5≤8) and is no longer feasible (2⋅0.5+2+3=6>5).

Notes

  • Integrality is essential. The strengthening exploits the gap between xk​=uk​−1 and xk​=uk​. For a continuous xk​ no such gap exists, and the constraint Ai⋅​x≤bi​ is already as tight as it can be in the LP.
  • When aik​=d. The new coefficient on xk​ becomes 0, so xk​ disappears from the row entirely. The constraint shrinks in support. This is more aggressive than just changing a number — it can trigger downstream singleton-column or implied-free substitutions.
  • When aik​<d. The bare strengthening formula would produce a negative coefficient on xk​, which is valid but unhelpful. In this case the original constraint is so loose at xk​=uk​−1 that bound strengthening (§3.2) on xk​ from the activity at xk​=uk​ gives a tighter result, so coefficient strengthening yields to that.
  • LP solvers cannot do this on their own. The strengthened constraint is a valid inequality for the integer-feasible region but not for the LP relaxation — it cuts off LP-feasible points. An LP solver, with no knowledge of integrality, has no reason to introduce it. The reduction is therefore an MIP-presolve speciality, valuable precisely because the LP relaxation gets stronger for free.
